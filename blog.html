Virtual Reality Innovation Through Realistic Physics-Based Games

Stagnant Game Innovation in a Growing Market

It is undeniable that virtual reality is growing rapidly. According to Grand View Research, the VR market is expected to grow at a compound annual rate of 18% from 2021 to 2028 [1]. The market size in 2021 was valued at $21.83 billion. It is estimated that in 2028 the market value will be roughly $69.60 billion.

While the market is certainly expanding with VR headset sales through the roof, it is arguable that innovation seen in VR games overall has stagnated. This is not to say that all developers are not innovating, but that the majority of popular VR games are not taking many interesting risks from a technical standpoint.

Pushing the Boundaries with Realistic Physics

Virtual reality serves a specific purpose in the gaming industry: to allow for realistic, immersive experiences that cannot be achieved through traditional video games. One could argue that many VR experiences are far from cutting edge as developers have often been succumbing to common immersion-breaking techniques (e.g. teleportation, floating hands). Understandably, not every game on the planet needs to be ground-breaking and exceptionally immersive, but it is evident that for a market experiencing drastic growth there is a lack of gameplay innovation.

So, how can VR developers begin to push the boundaries? One way is by creating games with realistic physics-based systems at their cores. The market has seen the success of Boneworks, Blade and Sorcery, and Half Life Alyx (among a handful of others), all of which contain realistic physics systems that elevate gameplay to new levels of immersion. These impressive successes have player-bases that emphasize what makes these games great: the realism.

Deterrents for Developers

You may be wondering why few developers are making games with realistic physics-based systems. If players are loving immersive physics experiences in games like Boneworks, Blade and Sorcery, and Half Life Alyx then why wouldn’t every developer be jumping on this opportunity? Why is it that not many VR games implement these systems?

To an extent, there is a higher barrier of entry when creating VR games with realistic physics-based systems. There is nothing easy about developing a VR game where classic Newtonian physics lay the foundation of every interaction experienced. Interactions like grabbing/throwing objects, firing weapons, and punching enemies can be achieved easily by “cutting corners,” resulting in aspects of realism being lost. However, if a developer implements physical interaction systems that stay as true as possible to Newtonian physics, an increased level of immersion is noticeable. One drawback of these physics systems is that they are potentially more likely to cause motion sickness in players, especially with locomotion mechanisms in which the player moves with a controller’s joystick and “smooth turns” an in-game character body with a second joystick. However, players have found that over time motion sickness is alleviated as the human body typically adjusts to the effects throughout the course of multiple play sessions.

Unfortunately, another reason for there being such a lack of physics-based games in VR is due to AAA game companies not taking more risks. Research and develop of new virtual reality game mechanics and techniques within major game corporations is far and few between. There are simply not enough VR games being released by AAA companies that can be deemed “cutting edge.” Valve’s Half Life Alyx is an outlier in a sea of “more of the same.” It is also worth mentioning that relatively small VR studios making new and interesting games are being bought up by companies like Oculus (Facebook). This is arguably a very bad thing as innovation can potentially be stifled by authoritative control over time.

Surreal Realism

Games with physics-based systems do not have to be so realistic that players feel like they are not even playing a game. Elements of VR physics-based games do not always have to stay true to our real reality. For instance, Blade and Sorcery contains fantasy elements with spell mechanics that allow players to throw fireballs, cast lightning, and control gravity. In Boneworks, a core mechanic known as “force grab” allows players to grab objects at an out-of-reach distance. These games also have a slow-motion feature that gives players the ability to slow down time when fighting enemies. At the end of the day, players want a gameplay experience, so VR games with realistic physics should certainly incorporate some interesting and surreal features (when appropriate).

A Future Standard

Undoubtedly, there is a new genre of VR games emerging. While physics-based games seem to be currently categorized under the “simulation” genre, it feels appropriate that these kinds of games should be tagged under a “realistic physics” or “physics-based” genre. Regardless of what you want to call the genre, realistic physics-based games are likely to become more popular.

The question is: How popular will physics-based VR games become? If one is to take the premise of virtual reality to be a medium of realistic, immersive gaming experiences, then one could agree that logically the answer to this question is “a hell of a lot more popular.” In the next few years, this genre of games will likely be a prevalent standard at the forefront of virtual reality.
